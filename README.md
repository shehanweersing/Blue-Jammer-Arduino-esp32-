üßê The Technical Challenge: Frequency HoppingBluetooth was specifically designed to be highly resilient against simple interference, making a jammer much more complex than just broadcasting noise.
1. Bluetooth Uses FHSS (Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum)
 The Problem: Unlike Wi-Fi, which often stays on one channel for a period, classic Bluetooth (BR/EDR) uses Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS). It rapidly switches among 79 separate channels in the 2.4 GHz band.
 The Speed: This hopping happens incredibly fast, typically 1,600 times per second.
 The Jammer's Dilemma: A simple jammer would have to spread its energy across all 79 channels simultaneously (called Barrage Jamming). This dilutes its power so much that it may not be strong enough to overpower a nearby Bluetooth device on any single channel.
